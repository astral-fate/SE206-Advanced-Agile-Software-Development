Key Concepts from Unit 1

Traditional SDLC vs. Agile Iterations

Traditional models like Waterfall follow a linear sequence: Requirements -> System Analysis -> Coding -> Testing -> Implementation -> Operations & Maintenance. Software is often delivered only at the very end after a long cycle.

Agile uses iterative cycles. Each iteration includes requirements analysis, design, development, and testing for a small subset of features, resulting in potentially shippable software increments released frequently.

Agile vs. Traditional: The Trade-off Triangle

This illustrates a fundamental difference in priorities:

Aspect Traditional Methodologies Agile Methodologies
Fixed Functionality / Scope Time (Iteration Length) & Resources (Team)
Flexible / Estimated Time & Resources Functionality / Scope

Agile prioritizes delivering value within fixed timeframes and budgets, allowing the scope to adapt, while traditional methods fix the scope upfront and estimate time/cost.

The Agile Manifesto (Values)

The core philosophy articulated in 2001, emphasizing:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

While there is value in the items on the right, Agile values the items on the left more.

The 12 Agile Principles

These principles elaborate on the Manifesto's values:

  1. Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
  2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage.
  3. Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference to the shorter timescale.
  4. Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.
  5. Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.
  6. The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a development team is face-to-face conversation.
  7. Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  8. Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
  9. Simplicity – the art of maximizing the amount of work not done – is essential.
  10. The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.
  11. (Implicit, often included) Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.
  12. (Implicit, often included) At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

Agile Characteristics

Agile development processes typically exhibit these traits:

  • Modularity: Work is broken down into manageable parts.
  • Iterative: Development occurs in short, repeated cycles (1-6 weeks).
  • Time-bound: Iterations have fixed durations.
  • Incremental: Value is delivered piece by piece, early and often.
  • Adaptive: Capable of responding to changes and new risks.
  • People-oriented: Values individuals, motivation, and trust.
  • Collaborative & Communicative: Emphasizes teamwork and open communication (especially face-to-face).
  • Self-organizing: Teams manage their own work.

Advantages of Agile

  • Early Value Realization: The incremental nature allows benefits to be seen early as the product develops.
  • Increased Visibility & Feedback: Small, frequent releases make progress visible to stakeholders, allowing for early issue identification.
  • Improved Responsiveness to Change: Easier to adapt to changing requirements or market conditions.
  • Enhanced Customer Satisfaction: Achieved through continuous delivery, collaboration, and responding to needs (aligns with Principle 1).
  • Focus on the Right Product: Collaboration and feedback loops help ensure the final product meets user needs.
